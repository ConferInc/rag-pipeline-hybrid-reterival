1. Feature Explanation (Marketing-Friendly)
When you look at a recipe, the system can summarize how well it lines up with familiar balanced-eating ideas—covering the main food types health authorities group together (such as vegetables, fruit, protein, dairy, and whole grains)—and can nudge you if the dish is heavy in some areas and light in others. If you need to change an ingredient—for example to avoid something you’re allergic to—the system suggests replacements in a sensible order: trusted pairings from a food knowledge base first, then closely related options from the catalog, with your allergies and diets applied so unsafe ideas are dropped. When nothing in the catalog fits, a language model proposes alternatives and explains them in plain language. Where nutrition is known, you see how the original and the swap compare so you can judge calories and protein yourself.

2. How It Works (Simple)
Recipe picture (insights)
The app looks at what’s in the recipe (by ingredient names) and maps those ingredients to broad food categories. It checks how many of the main categories are represented and, when the mix is narrow, it may suggest what kinds of foods could round out the meal.

Calories
When possible, it pulls calorie information for the recipe from structured nutrition data so you see energy content alongside the recipe.

Finding a swap
For a specific ingredient you want to replace, it first uses curated “this works instead of that” relationships, including short reasons (for example flavor or role in the dish).

If curated swaps aren’t enough
It searches the ingredient catalog for items that serve a similar role or read like good stand-ins.

Safety filters
It removes suggestions that don’t match your listed allergens or dietary rules, using structured links between ingredients and allergens or diet rules.

Last resort
If the catalog still has nothing suitable, a language model lists possible substitutes and short rationales, still respecting your allergy and diet constraints.

Nutrition comparison
When nutrition facts exist for both the original and the substitute, you get a side-by-side look at things like calories and protein so you can compare at a glance.

3. Why It’s Valuable
Benefits

You get plain-language balance feedback on a recipe instead of guessing whether it’s “complete.”
Swaps respect your allergies and diets instead of offering generic substitutions that could be unsafe.
Reasons and comparisons help you keep flavor and cooking intent in mind while changing ingredients.
Nutrition labels on originals vs. substitutes support informed choices (the system surfaces facts; it doesn’t hide the tradeoffs).
Problem it solves

Many people love a dish but can’t cook it as written. This ties together balanced-plate-style guidance on the recipe itself with practical replacement paths that stay within their health rules and help them see nutritional tradeoffs.

4. What Makes It Smart
Standards reflected (USDA-aligned food groups)
The product is built around the same major food groups used in current U.S. dietary guidance—protein, dairy, vegetables, fruits, and whole grains—and uses them to describe how varied a recipe is across those groups. Optional deeper guidance in the system can mirror priority themes from the 2025–2030 dietary guidelines (for example emphasizing protein and vegetables, sensible sugar and sodium awareness). That is advisory: it informs hints and ranking behavior where enabled, rather than blocking every “unbalanced” recipe.

What swaps do for the user
They turn a fixed recipe into something you can actually cook under your constraints, with short explanations where the data provides them.

How “AI” is used (in simple terms)

Matching ingredients when there is no hand-curated list: the system finds catalog items that fit the same kind of role as the original.
Fallback suggestions: when the catalog has no acceptable option, a language model writes a small set of substitute ideas and reasons, instructed to honor your allergies and diets.
Meal and recipe wording (where turned on): the same kind of model can be steered with balanced-eating priorities so suggestions don’t sound like “all carbs, no protein.”
Why it’s useful
It combines structured food knowledge (what contains allergens, what diets forbid, curated substitutes) with flexible suggestions when the database is incomplete—so you’re not stuck with an empty screen.

What makes it different (without overstating)

Layered strategy: curated first, catalog similarity second, generated ideas third—not only generic chat.
Safety-oriented filtering tied to your profile.
Recipe-level “insights” (category spread and optional balance hints) paired with ingredient-level swaps, which matches the marketing story—provided the app wires both surfaces together in the product.
